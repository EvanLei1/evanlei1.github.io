Speedometer

This speedometer is designed by putting it on images and videos that involve driving. In this example, the speedometer is used for a car, separating the speed into increments of 40 mph with a max speed of 160 mph. I also set up the speedometer in km/h and included yellow and red sections to alert you to the dangers of going at a very high speed. It also applies to trains but has three different categories with different max speeds. Unlike the car speedometer figure, the train speedometer figure does not have yellow or red sections. The speedometer diagram was used to simulate recorded videos and estimate photos.

The speedometer has three signal words: warning, danger, and peril. It only applies to cars, not trains. For the train speedometer, the bottom part was used for the target speed with a white background.

All caps were used for road conditions, while the exclamation mark was added for emergencies and sudden traffic slowdowns.

Signal word All caps Exclamation Notes
Caution No No Only used in specific road conditions, like bridge clearances
Warning Yes No If the speedometer is 20% above speed limit or at least 80 mph, or
if there are weather watches or advisories in the area
Danger Yes Yes If the speedometer is 40% above speed limit or at least 99 mph or
if there is a weather warning or traffic involving an accident or construction
Peril Yes Yes If the speedometer is 60% above speed limit or at least 124 mph or
if there is a road closure related to an accident or weather

Locations in France

In France, place names were sorted by canton, Arrondissement, department, and region. To prevent a long name, I omitted cantons and Arrondissements. There were 101 departments, 333 Arrondissements, and 2,054 cantons.

Since there were too many Arrondissements to show, the department Loire has three Arrondissements, 21 cantons, and 323 communes with a population of 769,029 and an area of 1,846 square miles. Instead of:

Ouches, Renaison, Roanne, Loire, Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes

We can use:

Ouches, Loire, Auvergne-Rhône-Alpes

Some communes were named after Arrondissements, so it was straightforward to communes like Melun.

Melun, Seine-et-Marne, Île-de-France

Some departments were named after rivers, while others were named after cities or names used in historical situations.

Locations in Japan

In Japan, place names were sorted by city, town, or village, followed by the prefecture. If you put Ii station, it will result in:

Ii station, Hagi, Yamaguchi

or

山口県萩市飯井駅

.

Otherwise, there are several places of the same name, so it was separated by city or prefecture, like Ōsaki (大崎) in Miyagi and Tokyo prefectures and Ōmiya (大宮) in Saitama and variants in Ibaraki and Miyazaki prefectures.

Due to confusion between Chinese and Japanese, the long vowel was added in some cases, like Hanyu Pinyin and Hanyū (羽生) in Saitama. Otherwise, the formatting looked like the same.

Kesennuma, Miyagi

or

宮城県気仙沼市

Location formatting in video games

Some games have locations. In Super Mario series, I simply put the location by using the world number and the level number, and I sometimes put names in it. In Kirby series, I have to put the level name and number. In Splatoon, I can put the stage name, and outside of the stages, I will put the location name.

Locations in the Philippines

In the Philippines, cities were sorted by [city name], [province]. The region and the baranguays were optional, like:

San Pablo, Laguna

In Manila and Quezon City, the word Philippines were used instead.

Santa Cruz, Manilla, Philippines

Quezon City, Philippines
